# Metric Guide

This guide explains the main metrics used by the generated reports in plain English. It is meant to help beginners read the report without removing the professional metrics.

## Price and Risk Metrics

| Metric | What It Means | Why It Matters | Common Beginner Mistake |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Total Return | How much the asset gained or lost over the selected period. | Shows the final result for that time window. | Assuming past return predicts future return. |
| CAGR | Annualized growth rate over the selected period. | Makes different time periods easier to compare. | Treating CAGR as a guaranteed future growth rate. |
| Max Drawdown | The largest fall from a previous peak. | Shows how painful the ride could have been. | Thinking the stock cannot fall more than its historical drawdown. |
| Volatility | How much the price moves around. | Higher volatility usually means more emotional pressure. | Thinking volatility is the same as permanent business risk. |
| Sharpe Ratio | Return earned per unit of volatility. | Helps judge whether returns were efficient. | Thinking a high Sharpe ratio means the stock is safe. |
| Sortino Ratio | Return compared with downside volatility. | Focuses more on harmful volatility. | Ignoring that it still uses historical price data. |
| Calmar Ratio | CAGR divided by max drawdown. | Compares return with the worst historical fall. | Treating it as a complete risk model. |
| Beta | How sensitive the stock is to the market or benchmark. | Beta above 1 usually means larger moves than the benchmark. | Thinking beta explains company-specific risk. |
| Alpha | Return beyond what beta would imply. | Helps estimate excess performance versus the benchmark. | Treating alpha as skill or future edge without deeper analysis. |
| Tracking Error | How much the stock's returns differ from the benchmark. | Shows how different the ride is from the benchmark. | Thinking lower tracking error is always better. |
| Information Ratio | Excess return divided by tracking error. | Measures benchmark outperformance per unit of active risk. | Ignoring whether the period is long enough to be meaningful. |

## Business Quality Metrics

| Metric | What It Means | Why It Matters | Common Beginner Mistake |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Revenue | Money the company receives from selling products or services. | Shows business scale. | Thinking revenue growth automatically means profit growth. |
| Gross Margin | Revenue left after direct costs. | Shows pricing power and product economics. | Comparing margins across very different industries. |
| Operating Margin | Profit left after normal operating expenses. | Shows whether the business model works after running costs. | Ignoring one-time expenses or accounting changes. |
| Net Income | Accounting profit after all expenses. | Shows whether the company is profitable on paper. | Assuming net income always equals cash generation. |
| Operating Cash Flow | Cash generated by core operations. | Helps test whether profits turn into cash. | Ignoring working-capital effects. |
| Free Cash Flow | Cash left after capital expenditures. | Supports buybacks, debt reduction, dividends, and reinvestment. | Treating one strong year as a permanent trend. |

## Ruin Risk Metrics

| Metric | What It Means | Why It Matters | Common Beginner Mistake |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Net Debt | Total debt minus cash. | Shows whether the company is net debt or net cash. | Ignoring debt maturity and interest rates. |
| EBITDA | A rough proxy for operating earning power before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. | Often used to compare debt burden with earning power. | Treating EBITDA as real free cash flow. |
| Net Debt / EBITDA | How many years of EBITDA would roughly cover net debt. | Higher values can signal balance-sheet pressure. | Using it for companies with negative or unstable EBITDA. |
| Debt / FCF | Total debt compared with free cash flow. | Shows whether cash generation can support debt. | Ignoring that FCF can be cyclical or one-off. |
| Cash Runway | How long cash might last if the company is burning cash. | Important for unprofitable companies. | Assuming the burn rate stays constant. |

## Valuation Metrics

| Metric | What It Means | Why It Matters | Common Beginner Mistake |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| PE | Price divided by earnings. | Shows how much investors pay for current earnings. | Thinking low PE always means cheap. |
| Forward PE | Price divided by expected future earnings. | Reflects market expectations for future profit. | Forgetting that estimates can be wrong. |
| Price to Sales | Market value compared with revenue. | Useful when earnings are low or negative. | Comparing software and retailers as if margins were the same. |
| Price to Book | Market value compared with book equity. | Often more relevant for banks and asset-heavy companies. | Using it blindly for asset-light companies. |
| EV / Revenue | Enterprise value compared with revenue. | Includes debt and cash in the valuation frame. | Ignoring margin differences between companies. |
| EV / EBITDA | Enterprise value compared with EBITDA. | Common valuation multiple for operating businesses. | Forgetting that EBITDA is not free cash flow. |

## Reading Rule

No single metric is enough.

Use metrics as evidence in a chain:

```text
Business model -> growth quality -> cash conversion -> balance sheet risk -> valuation -> benchmark opportunity cost
```
