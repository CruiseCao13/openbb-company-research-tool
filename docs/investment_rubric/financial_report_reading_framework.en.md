# Financial Report Reading Framework

The core of financial report analysis is to judge whether a company can consistently generate cash flow.

This framework is for understanding companies. It is not investment advice, does not provide buy, sell, or hold recommendations, and does not provide target prices.

## 1. Business Model

Start with how the company makes money. Identify the product or service, the customer, why the customer pays, whether revenue is recurring or one-time, and whether growth comes from durable demand, pricing, market expansion, cycles, acquisitions, or accounting effects.

If the business model is unclear, revenue, profit, cash flow, and valuation should remain screening observations rather than firm conclusions.

## 2. Revenue Growth

Revenue growth asks whether the company is getting bigger. Check current revenue, year-over-year growth, quarter-over-quarter change, guidance, and expectation gaps.

Growth is not automatically good. The reader must understand whether growth comes from volume, price, mix, customer expansion, acquisition, subsidy, commodity cycle, or one-time items.

## 3. Gross Margin

Gross margin asks whether growth has quality. High or improving gross margin can suggest product quality, cost control, pricing power, or better mix. Declining gross margin can point to competition, cost pressure, weaker mix, or underutilized capacity.

Gross margin must fit the sector. Banks and insurers should not be forced into an ordinary manufacturing gross-margin framework.

## 4. Operating Profit

Operating profit asks whether the core business is getting stronger. Focus on operating income, operating margin, expense ratios, and operating leverage.

If revenue grows but operating profit does not, check sales expense, R&D, G&A, subsidies, depreciation, impairment, and one-time items.

## 5. Net Profit

Net profit asks whether the company retains earnings after all costs. Review net income, EPS, dilution, minority interest, and one-time items.

Net profit should not be read alone. It should be checked against operating profit, cash flow, and the balance sheet to avoid mistaking accounting gains for operating strength.

## 6. Cash Flow

Cash flow asks whether the company can fund itself through operations. Focus on operating cash flow, capex, free cash flow, and financing need.

A good business usually converts profit into cash. If profit grows while operating cash flow is weak, check receivables, inventory, contract liabilities, prepayments, capex, and financing activity.

## 7. Balance Sheet

The balance sheet asks whether the company is financially resilient. Review cash, debt, current assets, current liabilities, inventory, receivables, goodwill, and other assets that may be impaired.

The balance sheet is not static. It shows whether the company can withstand cycles, expansion, losses, tighter financing, or operating mistakes.

## 8. Key Business Metrics

Key business metrics explain the financial results. Different companies require different metrics: users, average revenue per user, retention, production volume, sales volume, orders, capacity utilization, NIM, NPL ratio, premium income, loss ratio, or drug pipeline progress.

Metrics must connect to revenue, profit, and cash flow. Otherwise they are decorative.

## 9. Guidance

Guidance asks what management indicates about the future. Review revenue guidance, margin guidance, full-year outlook, capex plans, and management commentary.

Guidance is not a promise. It must be judged against the company’s history, industry cycle, competition, and current financial data.

## 10. Market Expectations

Market expectations ask whether results differed from expectations. Compare actual results with market expectations, beat or miss, and expectation revisions.

If expectation data is unavailable, do not invent market reaction. Mark it as a data gap.

## 11. Valuation

Valuation asks whether the current price already prices in too much future growth. Check whether the valuation method fits the company, what growth is implied, whether a margin of safety exists, and what downside appears if growth misses.

Valuation is not a trading conclusion. It describes what the current price requires from the future and cannot by itself generate an investment recommendation.
